Have you ever seen a woman walking home at night with her keys clenched between her fingers? Not because she's forgetful — but because she's scared.

It's not paranoia. It's preparation.

This is one of the many things women carry every day. Not just books and bags — but fear. Awareness. The burden of being told, "Be careful," instead of the world being told, "Don't hurt."

Gender inequality is not always loud. It's not just about laws or slogans. Sometimes, it's about the daily dance women and gender minorities perform just to feel safe, seen, or respected.

A crowded bus, a teenage girl clutches her bag tighter, turns away from a man who's standing too close. She war wants to speak up, but doesn't. Because we taught her to stay quiet — not him to back off.

In an office, a woman's idea ignored.

Minutes later, a man repeats it and gets applause. She smiles anyway, because she's been taught that being "too assertive" makes her "unpleasant."

In school, a young gay student lowers his voice when speaking in public. He's learned the hard way that his softness makes him a target.

These are not isolated events. They are patterns. Patterns so common that they become invisible – unless you're the one living them

We need to rethink how we define inequality.

It's not just about numbers or salaries.

It's in who feels safe walking home.

Who's interrupted more in meetings.

Who's expected to "adjust," to "wait their turn," to "prove their worth."

We teach girls to protect themselves — but not boys to unlearn entitlement.

We say "all genders are equal" — but laugh at jokes that reduce women to punchlines, or call effeminate boys weak.

We post #Empowerment — but ignore the subtle sexism in our own homes, classrooms, and conversations.

So what do we do?

We start seeing what's always been there.

We speak when it's inconvenient.

We believe people when they say they're uncomfortable — even when it doesn't seem like a big deal to us.

Because it is a big deal to them.

Gender inequality doesn't always wear a banner or hold a protest sign.

Sometimes, it walks quietly beside you, waiting for you to notice.

"And when you finally do — don't look away."
